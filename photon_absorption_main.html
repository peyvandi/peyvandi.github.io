Light Absorption by a Population of Interleaved Photoreceptors

by Shahram Peyvandi


Summary

For an image captured by a digital camera, the histogram distribution of colors is obtained easily by the number of pixels within a specific range of digital outputs. What does the histogram distribution of a retinal image in the eye look like when observing colors? We characterize spatiotemporal variation of absorbed light energy in individual cells based on optical specifications of the eye and retinal receptors. Whereas the conventional assumption of equal excitations in like-type cells implies a single chromaticity for a uniform color stimulus, our work suggests instead a distribution of responses within the cone excitation space. Our findings help understanding the fundamental factors of retinal stimulation that impact human color perception such as color detection and discrimination.

Distribution of Photons of a Single-Frequency among a Population of Cells


The electrical activity in a retinal receptor is initiated when photons are absorbed by photopigment molecules densely packed within the outer segment disk membranes of the cell. The actual number of photons absorbed in a cell varies with Poisson fluctuation when the receptor is exposed to a photon flux of a particular wavelength. This fluctuation introduces a spatial variation in absorption by individual cells. It means that two identical cells of the same exact type exposed to the same photon flux absorb different numbers of photons.




Consider a case in which identical photoreceptors are exposed to a uniform monochromatic light. If photon absorptions in each cell fluctuate with Poisson statistics, then two identical receptors may absorb different numbers of photons.

Distribution of Multi-Wavelength Light Energy among a Population of Cells


In case where the stimulating light has a broad spectrum at different wavelengths, due to the dependency of photon energy on wavelength and the principle of univariance, we concern the distribution of cells among levels of absorbed light energy.



Identical photoreceptors, exposed to a uniform multi-wavelength light, absorb different energy levels (spatial variation). The number of cells at a given level of absorbed energy fluctuates with repeated exposure (temporal variation).
